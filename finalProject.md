DELIVERABLES AND EVALUATION:
- GitHub repository (40%): all the code, the math development, explanations, clear structure and reproducibility.
- 2-page LaTeX report submitted as PDF (20%): written like a short conference abstract (introduction, method, results, discussion).
- Pre-recorded 12-minute presentation (40%): slides shared via a link (YouTube or similar).

For the 12-min presentation, imagine a scientific conference. Depending on your project, you might want to answer questions like: What is the problem? Why is it important? What is currently done? What do you propose? What methodology did you use? What are your results and challenges? A clear, well-structured, well-practiced presentation will receive a higher grade than one that is unclear. Do not overload it with formulas or fine details (these can go in the GitHub repository), rather try to do a good use of the time and provide a nice overview that could be follow by your colleagues in class.

SOME POSSIBLE DIRECTIONS:
a) Pick a problem either discussed in class, or assigned as homework, or one you personally find interesting, and extend it further. For example: harmonic oscillators (ideal, damped, and with a force), Lotka–Volterra, van der Pol oscillator, Rössler attractor, Lorenz system, double pendulum, other nonlinear or chaotic systems, a particular system from classical mechanics, etc. You could analyze the mathematics and qualitative behavior, derive or discuss analytical solutions (when possible), study numerical stability, compare different numerical solvers, and compare different machine learning approaches such as baseline neural networks, ResNets, Neural ODEs, Lagrangian/Hamiltonian Networks, PINNs and/or PINOs whenever applicable.

b) Pick a paper in Scientific Machine Learning (for example, any deep learning for solving ODEs, Neural ODEs, Lagrangian or Hamiltonian Networks, Physics-Informed Neural Networks, neural operators, including also works written by researchers in the field such as George Karniadakis or Souvik Chakraborty). You may analyze their methodology and either replicate some or all of their results if possible and not too intricate or either apply that methodology to other simpler problems otherwise.

c) For students who are already working on a research project (e.g., many PhD candidates or departmental collaborators), it may be more efficient to build on your existing work. You can incorporate Scientific Machine Learning concepts into your current project, motivate why they are relevant, present preliminary results, and explore the topic more deeply so that what you learned in this course directly contributes to your research.
